Yesterday at The Knowledge Society (TKS), Farza Majeed came to give a talk, and his story completely shifted how I think about ideas and action.

He began by saying that you have to start from somewhere. Too often, we wait for the “perfect idea” or the perfect plan, but in reality, ideas only grow when you take action. The first idea you have is rarely the final one, but it’s the one that gets you moving, and that movement is what creates progress.

He shared his experience building an AR Menu app. He spent months perfecting every detail before showing it to anyone. But in the end, no one was really interested, and all that time was basically wasted.

From that experience, he taught us about “toy versions.” A toy version is a small, simple version of your idea that captures its core concept. It doesn’t need to be polished or impressive; it’s meant to be shown to people, get feedback, and learn what works and what doesn’t. Farza also emphasized that if he had created a “toy” version of the AR Menu app and shared it earlier, he would have learned so much faster and avoided wasting months.

Hearing this story made me realize how often I overcomplicate things, refining ideas before even seeing how the world responds. Clarity and progress come from taking action, testing, and iterating. A toy version isn’t just a prototype; it’s a tool to turn hesitation into growth.

My biggest takeaway: when I have an idea, the next step isn’t to polish it endlessly, it’s to build a toy version, share it, gather feedback, and let that guide the next move. Farza’s story reminded me that progress comes from doing, failing, and learning, not waiting for perfection.

Big thanks to The Knowledge Society (TKS), Sahil Arora, and Nadia Rajab, PhD, for the opportunity to experience such an inspiring talk.

↳ Let users fix things themselves

↳ They don't care how old I am - they care if it works
