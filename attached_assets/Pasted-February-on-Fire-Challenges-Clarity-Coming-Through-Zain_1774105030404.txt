What I Worked On This Month
If January was about endurance, February was about execution.

The biggest thing consuming my time and energy this month, and honestly, the past four weeks leading into it , was a challenge I did through TKS in collaboration with Lovable (yes, THE lovable). This wasn’t a school project or a casual side thing. This was real. We were given an actual problem the company is currently facing, and our job was to come up with a real solution.

For weeks, my team and I were deep in it, researching, validating, iterating, going back and forth, and genuinely pouring everything we had into it. There were days I sacrificed other things because I knew this mattered. It wasn’t always pretty, and it definitely wasn’t always easy, but we kept moving.

And then, towards the end of February, we found out.

Out of every TKS team globally, across cities, countries, and hundreds of students, only 19 teams made it through. Our project was one of them. And out of all the teams from Dubai, only three got selected. We’re one of those three.

What does that mean? Our work is now being reviewed directly by Lovable’s executive team. Not a junior panel, not a preliminary round — the executive team. If they like what they see, we get to present to them directly. Face to face. That possibility alone is something I couldn’t have imagined at the start of this month.

I’m not going to pretend it hasn’t sunk in yet, because it has. I’m proud. But more than the result, I’m proud of the process, the weeks of focus, the late nights, the moments where I had to choose between what was comfortable and what mattered. That’s what got us here.

Outside of that, I also continued working on my Replicate project and started exploring UI/UX design, diving into Figma, studying design psychology, and thinking seriously about what makes a product not just functional, but something people actually want to use.

Harvard Business Case Study — TKS Session

This one genuinely surprised me. I didn’t expect to love it as much as I did, but here we are.

The case was on Starbucks’ growth strategy in China. We were handed a 23-page document, exhibits, data, and real business context, and had to dig through it, make sense of it, and come up with recommendations. My first-ever business case study.

What I didn’t expect was how much I’d enjoy the analytical side of it, not formula-based math, but the kind where you have to hunt for the right numbers, figure out which metrics actually matter, and then use them to make a real argument. That’s the kind of problem-solving I love getting into.

I also walked away knowing things I genuinely didn’t know before, like what GDP actually means, what data points businesses actually use to make decisions, and how much strategy goes into something that looks simple on the surface. It opened up a whole new way of thinking about the world for me.

“Build Your First App with AI” — Farza & Julian | February 28th

On the very last day of the month, I joined a live YouTube workshop hosted by Farza and Julian Alvarez, a 2.5-hour interactive session where we built our first web app using AI, together, in real time.

There was a Discord running alongside it, questions flying everywhere, and it was one of those rare sessions where you’re learning and doing at the exact same time.

The lesson that stuck with me: doing teaches better than studying. You could spend weeks watching videos about how to build an app. You could read every article, every tutorial, every breakdown. Or you could just build one, and in two hours, learn more than you would have in those weeks. That’s not to say learning theory is useless. But action accelerates understanding in a way that passive learning simply can’t match.

Stories, Experiences & Lessons Learned

I made the school basketball team this month.

For context: I’ve been playing football for as long as I can remember. But recently, I made a deliberate decision, I wanted to shift that energy into basketball and actually become good at it. I put in the work, and when the opportunity came to try out, getting selected was the expected outcome. And I did, I made the team.

Mindsets of This Month
Exceeding Expectations
There’s a version of doing things where you meet the bar. You do what’s asked, you check the box, you move on. And then there’s the version where you ask yourself, what would it look like if I took this further than anyone expected?

That question changes everything. It’s not about perfectionism or burning yourself out. It’s about refusing to settle for “good enough” when you know you’re capable of something better. This month, especially with the Lovable challenge, that mindset is what separated the work we did from the work we could’ve done. The result reflected that.

Stoicism
Stoicism is one of those things that sounds old and philosophical until you actually understand what it’s saying, and then it becomes one of the most practical frameworks you can have.

At its core, Stoicism is about one simple idea: there are things you control, your effort, your attitude, your response. And there are things you don’t, other people’s opinions, outcomes, the world around you. Stoicism says: put all your energy into the first category, and genuinely let go of the second.

Think of it like being a sailor. You can’t control the wind. You can’t control the waves. But you can control how you set the sail. A Stoic doesn’t panic when a storm hits; they focus on what they can actually do, right now, in this moment.

This hit me hard in February. There were weeks where stress came from things completely outside my control — teachers, timing, circumstances, things just not going the way I planned. And every time I caught myself spiraling over something I couldn’t change, Stoicism was the anchor. Not in a cold, emotionless way — but in a way that brought me back to: okay, what can I actually do here?

It’s become one of my favorite mental frameworks. Not because it makes hard things easy, but because it makes hard things navigable.

Self-Discipline
Discipline is what shows up when motivation doesn’t.

This month taught me that more clearly than ever. There were days I didn’t want to work. Days when I was tired, drained, or just not feeling it. But I had commitments to my team, to my projects, and even to myself. And discipline is what kept me moving on those days.

Motivation is a feeling. It comes and goes. Discipline is a decision. It compounds. And the months that matter, the ones where you actually make progress, are almost always built on discipline more than inspiration.
